## Key Findings

The Fama-French three-factor model was used to estimate factor exposures for SPY, QQQ and MELI using daily data from 2015 onwards.

### Market Exposure

All assets show strong exposure to the market factor.

- SPY beta ≈ 0.98
- QQQ beta ≈ 1.11
- MELI beta ≈ 1.34

This indicates that MELI behaves as a high-beta stock, amplifying overall market movements.

### Size Factor (SMB)

SPY and QQQ show negative SMB loadings, consistent with their exposure to large-cap stocks.

MELI exhibits a positive SMB loading, suggesting behavior closer to small-cap growth stocks.

### Value vs Growth (HML)

The HML factor reveals strong growth exposure.

- SPY is neutral.
- QQQ has a significant growth tilt.
- MELI displays an extreme growth profile with a large negative HML beta.

### Model Fit

The explanatory power of the model varies across assets.

- SPY: R² ≈ 0.99
- QQQ: R² ≈ 0.95
- MELI: R² ≈ 0.38

This indicates that MELI's returns contain a large idiosyncratic component not captured by standard risk factors.
### Rolling Market Exposure

The rolling beta analysis reveals that MELI's exposure to the market factor has varied significantly over time.

During the 2017–2018 period the market beta exceeded 2.5, indicating extremely high sensitivity to market movements typical of high-growth technology firms.

Following the pandemic period the beta gradually declined and recently fell below 1, suggesting that MELI may be transitioning toward a more mature risk profile.

### Factor Contribution

The cumulative factor contribution analysis for QQQ shows that most of the excess return is explained by exposure to the market factor.

A secondary contribution arises from the HML factor, reflecting the ETF's strong tilt toward growth stocks.

The SMB factor plays a minimal role, consistent with the large-cap composition of the Nasdaq 100.
## CAPM vs Fama-French 3

To evaluate whether additional risk factors improve explanatory power, I compared the CAPM against the Fama-French three-factor model.

The results show that:

- SPY is already well explained by the market factor alone, so the improvement from FF3 is limited.
- QQQ benefits more clearly from the FF3 specification, mainly because the HML factor captures its strong growth tilt.
- MELI shows only partial improvement, suggesting that a substantial share of its returns is driven by idiosyncratic firm-specific shocks rather than broad systematic factors.

This comparison highlights why multi-factor models are often more informative than CAPM when analyzing growth-oriented assets.
